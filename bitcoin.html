MORALITY OF ENERGY USE

A look at Greenpeace and Ripple's campaign to "Change the Code".

Set aside Bitcoin's use as the most secure, permissionless, decentralized monetary network the world has ever seen for now. Simply taking a look at energy, we have been taught that wasting energy is not good: "turn the light off when you leave", "shut the front door, you're letting all the heat out", "shut off the engine you're goin to waste gas". Efficient energy use is important, one should arguably not use more than they need to complete a certain task. It doesn't make economic sense, and some argue it doesn’t make moral sense. 'Finish your plate, people are starving in Africa”.

Funny that you mention Africa. Compared to members of the highest GDP members of the Western and Eastern Worlds which comprises the Global North, the Global South uses a fraction of the energy both nominal and per capita. Energy consumption has correlations with positive outcomes including reduction of food scarcity, increased economic participation, and disaster survivability. Our modern world, and its winners are decided by use of energy.

In December of 2022, Jack Dorsey, former CEO of Twitter and now bitcoin-focused financial services company Block, partnered with Gridless Compute to stand up a small-scale hydroelectric generation facility in Kenya which through the use of colocated bitcoin mining, provides 500 families with local affordable energy for the first time, cutting the rate by more than half at bitcoin's current mining conditions. The protocol’s direct tie to energy makes this possible. This configuration can be replicated throughout Africa, Latin America and Southeast Asia, targeting stranded energy as the "buyer of first resort". This is one of the most noble of energy uses I can think of, wouldn't you agree? Perhaps in time, incentivized build out of stranded renewable energy across and by the Global South will allow for thriving communities that can escape the historic oppression of former colonial nations.

Let us also look at bitcoin as a "buyer of last resort" that is, a buyer for already consumed energy would otherwise be wasted due to inability to be transferred, sold, or otherwise used by the market. Again, we do not care about bitcoin as an asset, simply how its mining equipment functions in a "good" use of energy.

A recent study on the scale of methane emissions was published which highlighted the immense amount of leakage that still occurs despite flaring practices being required among the Western World. The burning of excess natural gas is done because energy generators have to be able to provide an amount of power that is demanded at any moment, in order to keep our civilization running, but are unable to put gas back into the ground once tapped for that base load amount. Despite best efforts, wind moving across the flare stacks and efficiency limits of the practice still allow leakage of methane, which is 80 times more warming than carbon dioxide during the most concentrated time in the atmosphere.

Enter colocated bitcoin mining, where they are capturing that waste methane, and running it through a generator to negate methane emissions, using that energy for their own purposes, but having the additional benefits of providing load-balancing to the grid, and unprecedented mitigation of one of the most damaging GHG to the atmosphere. Companies such as Vespene Energy are also targeting methane outgassing from landfills, particularly those of smaller scale that are unable to afford expensive methane recapture systems. Currently, Bitcoin is on a path to reduce warming by 0.5 degrees celsius, or 33 percent of the IPCC"s goal, this side of the century. This would buy us more time, something climate experts would agree is direly needed.

As you can see, energy used in appropriate ways can be net beneficial to people and their environment. Proof of work is the foundation of life, and energy is a necessary component of that. We should celebrate a technology which allows for more efficient use and conservation of our resources.



CALLING OUT DEFECTIVE ALTRUISM

A look at Greenpeace and Ripple's campaign to "Change the Code".

Near the beginning of 2022, Twitter account @CleanUpBitcoin entered the scene on a mission: convince environmentally conscious consumers that Bitcoin uses "too much energy" and that the quick and easy solution to "Change the Code" would put Bitcoin on the right path. Their concerns are not new, in fact a number of Bitcoiners (myself included) had this same moral quandary themselves. There is no dodging it, Bitcoin uses energy measuring in Terawatts, no small amount. The argument predicates itself on a few assumptions:

1. Energy use is unilaterally harmful and should be curtailed.

2. An edit to Bitcoin's source code will force the millions of nodes running the software to update to the edited version.

3. Bitcoin provides no use that something else that uses less energy can not do.

Before we begin to dismantle these points, it is important to disclose some context. The Change the Code campaign was funded by Chris Larsen, CEO of Ripple, who created and awarded themselves a majority of the cryptocurrency they created, XRP. Ripple, the centralized corporation aligned with the "too big to fail" banking industry, is currently under investigation by the United Stated government Securities and Exchange Commision. Chris Larsen donated $5 million USD to Greenpeace in what appears to be an effort to thwart its greatest competitor, an open source protocol voluntarily adopted by millions. This amount is reported to be around half of the subsidiary responsible for the campaign, GreenpeaceUSA's annual operating budget. Chris Larsen would accumulate a return of billions of dollars if his campaign was successful. The account has gone so far as to delete its own tweets that were discovered to highlight the positive effects of Bitcoin's energy use.



WASTE NOT, WANT NOT

#mine4heat: rethinking waste in heating applications.



Bitcoin is an open network that allows users to acquire, store, and transact value in a way that is permissionless, without an intermediary, and with minimized trust between either party. Specialized computers, Bitcoin ASICs which are commonly called "miners", work to batch these transactions and insert them into the globally distributed ledger. Miner operators are incentivised to find the cheapest sources of energy, which increasingly are wasted, stranded, renewable and GHG emission mitigating sources.

Like all computers, the power (measured in Watts) required to compute, known in bitcoin as hashpower, is efficiently transformed into heat (measured in BTU). This heat byproduct can be captured and used to perform additional work. Miner operators around the world have begin to capture and make use of their computer equipment's thermal energy to heat their homes, pools, greenhouses and even aquatic fish ponds.

Often described as "wasted energy", it is becoming increasingly clear that traditional radiant heating is wasteful in that the same amount of Watts, directly related to BTUs, could additionally be securing the hardest monetary network in existience. As we continue to devise new ways to utilize Bitcoin's close ties to energy, what will the world look like in another 14 years, 7 halvings into the monetary network's lifespan. Time (and energy) will tell.
